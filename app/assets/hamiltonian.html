Hamiltonian Monte Carlo introduces an auxiliary momentum variable \boldsymbol{v} and draws from a joint density p(\boldsymbol{v}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = p(\boldsymbol{v} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})

In most applications of HMC, the auxiliary momentum variable does not depend on the parameters \boldsymbol{\theta}.

The joint density p(\boldsymbol{v}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) defines the Hamiltonian \begin{aligned} \mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{v}) &= - \log p(\boldsymbol{v}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) \\ & = - \log p(\boldsymbol{v} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) - \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \\ & = - \log p(\boldsymbol{v}) - \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \\ & = K(\boldsymbol{v}) + U(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \end{aligned}

where the simplification between lines 2 and 3 occur because the momentum does not depend on the parameters.

The Hamiltonian represents the total energy in a system: the sum of the the kinetic energy K(\boldsymbol{v}), which depends on the momentum, and the potential energy U(\boldsymbol{\theta}), which depends on the position \boldsymbol{\theta}.

In our use case, we take \begin{aligned} U(\boldsymbol{\theta}) &= -\log[p^*(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \boldsymbol{y})] \\ K(\boldsymbol{v}) &= \frac{1}{2} \boldsymbol{v}^T \boldsymbol{v} + \text{const} \end{aligned}
